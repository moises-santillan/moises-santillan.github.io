Evolution of the COVID-19 epidemic in Mexico

Moisés Santillán
Spanish version

Methodology and additional data

Here, the evolution of the COVID-19 epidemic in Mexico is analyzed via the daily incidence of hospital admissions, organized according the symptom onset date. Although it is possible to get the daily incidence of confirmed cases from the databases published by Mexico's Secretaría de Salud (Ministry of Health), it is not a good indicator of the epidemic evolution in the country. The reason is that Mexico makes use of the so-called sentinel model to track the epidemic. According to this model, a few thousands of sentinel health units, distributed across the country, are in charge of collecting samples from suspicious patients (those having COVID-19-like symptoms). In principle, samples are collected from all patients demanding hospitalization, but only from 10% of ambulatory patients. However, a deper analysis of the reported data reveals that the fraction of patients that demand hospitalization greatly varies along time, and from place to place. While facing similar problems, other people have decided to use the incidence of deaths as an indicator of the epidemic evolution. However, the number of deaths in some Mexican states is too small for statistical purposes. Hence, I decided to compute and use the incidence of hospital admissions because, in principle, all of them are recorded by the sentinel model, everywhere in the country.

The processing of information in Mexico's sentinel model is as follows. Once a sample has been collected from the patient, the corresponding record is included into the database published daily by the Ministry of Health. Among other pieces of information, each record includes the following data: place of birth, place of residence, age, gender, presence of comorbidities, symptom onset date, date of registry, date of death, and test result. Initially, the test result field is set as pending, and it is updated as either positive or negative a few days later, once the sample has been analyzed. Regarding the date of death field, it is initially left empty, and it is updated if the patient eventually dies. A database with all the records collected in such a way is updated daily in the following website. https://bit.ly/3gnaMvI. Although the date of confirmation of each record is not published, it can be computed by contrasting databases published in consecutive days. I have done so for all the records with positive results, and the current version of the database thus amended can be found in the following GitHub repository: https://github.com/moises-santillan/covid-19-mexico. There, I also publish the time series of positive cases, deaths, and hospital admissions (for the country and each of its states), organized according to the symptom onset date. There are some known inconsistencies due to some records being eliminated and the same ID number being used again. They can be detected because the symptom onset date is posterior to the date of confirmation. Other known inconsistencies are records with symptom onset dates prior to February 15th.

As everywhere else, there is a delay between the onset of symptoms and the confirmation of a positive case. In Mexico, this delay is 10 days long in average. Due to this delay, there is an underreporting in the number of cases with recent symptom onset dates. Since all tested patients are recorded into the database even before their test results are available, this can be used to partially correct the underreporting. What one has to do is to extrapolate to the present day the value the positivity ratio had 20 days in the past, and multiply times the total number of applied tests, organized according to the symptom onset date. The positivity ratio is defined as the fraction of tests, applied to patients whose symptoms started on a given date, that resulted positive. Due to the discussed underreporting, the positivity ratio computed for dates more recent than 20 days in the past is not accurate. Once this correction has been applied, the resulting data still suffers from underreporting in recent dates, do to the delay between the onset of symptoms and the registration of a case in the database. To correct it, one can follow the Robert Koch Institute (RKI) methodology, which consists in using the computed cumulative probability distribution of time delays, that can be interpreted as the fraction of cases that are confirmed, as a function of the number of days that have passed since the onset of symptoms. This process of estimating the current value of confirmed cases is known as nowcasting. In the graphs below, I plot both the daily incidence of hospital admissions, as reported by the Ministry of Health, and the nowcasting-resulting value, as a function of the symptom onset date.

Finally, the effective reproductive number (Re) is computed by means of the RKI method. In this article different methods for computing Re are contrasted.

Analysis on the epidemic in the country and its states

The daily incidence of hospital admissions in Mexico is plotted in Figure 1, together with the nowcasting estimated values, and the effective reproductive number. The various plots in this graph can be removed and included back again by clicking on the color symbols located in the figure right margin.

By means of the dropdown menu, located in the top right corner of Figure 1, it is possible to display the graphs corresponding to the epidemic evolution in each one of Mexico's states. There, we can see that the epidemic behavior has been quite heterogeneous both in intensity and in timing. The epidemic started at different times, and has evolved at a different pace, in different places. In some cases, it is difficult to appreciate the epidemic tendency due to the error bars. When this happens, one can remove the plot corresponding to the reported data. To have a joint view of the epidemic current state in Mexico, the normalized average daily incidence of hospital admissions, averaged over the penultimate week and normalized to the population size, is plotted in Figure 2 for all the Mexican states, as well as for the country itself.

Figure 2 gives a picture of the epidemic current state. However, it is also important to know the tendency. This information is given by the effective reproductive number (Re). Values larger than one mean that the contagion rate is increasing. In Figure 3, a plot of the the value that Re had two weeks ago is presented for all the Mexican states, as well as for the country.
